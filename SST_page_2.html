SST Reconstruction

Relative offsets Absolute biases Location errors

To account for heterogeneous SST biases, a linear-mixed-effect (LME) method has been developed to intercompare nearby measurements from different subsets of data and adjust individual subsets to be consistent with one another. However, the LME method is not designed to inform absolute biases common to all groups, whose effect is non-negligible at global or century-long timescales.

The correction of common SST biases was treated to be independent to continental temeprature records. In other words, these widely-used existing datasets of surface temperatues do not demonstrate consistency between land and ocean temperatures, particularly before the mid-20th Century. 

We examine the local relationship between LATs and SSTs along coastlines using station LAT data from GHCNv4, SST reports from ICOADS3, and a coupled energy-balance model for inferring SSTs from LATs.  Inferred coastal-mean SSTs are 0.2°C warmer than indicated by bucket-based SST records from 1900 to 1940 but 0.1°C cooler before 1900.  This local discrepancy recapitulates the discrepancy between global LATs and SSTs and confirms that the global discrepancy is non-physical.  Using diurnal amplitudes of observed SSTs, we show that the discrepancy at least partly arises from under-corrected SST biases associated with systematic changes in the types of buckets used in the late 19th century. 

We use LATs to infer a correction for SSTs while propagating uncertainties in LATs through to the SST correction. Corrected SSTs show continuous warming of 0.07°C/decade ([0.06, 0.08]) from 1880 to 1945.  Combined LAT and SST estimates of GMST show warming of 1.2--1.5°C since the 1880s (full range among 200 members), with the error reflecting mainly uncertainties in the homogenization of LATs.  Our results of historical warming imply that global warming may have already reached a 1.5°C threshold.  More generally, our findings demonstrate systematic errors in historical SSTs and highlight the importance of developing a consistent land-sea temperature dataset spanning the 20th century for documenting change, bench-marking models, and guiding mitigation and adaptation strategies.
