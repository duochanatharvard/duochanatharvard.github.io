SST Reconstruction

Relative offsets Absolute biases

To account for heterogeneous SST biases, a linear-mixed-effect (LME) method has been developed to intercompare nearby measurements from different subsets of data and adjust individual subsets to be consistent with one another. However, the LME method is not designed to inform absolute biases common to all groups, whose effect is non-negligible at global or century-long timescales.

To account for common biases, we propose a statistical framework to correct absolute biases for individual subsets. Compared with the LME method that aims at homogenizing relative offsets between groups, the framework introduced on this page also involves referencing SSTs to air temperatures from coastal and island stations.

Applying the methodology to more than 100 million ship-based SSTs from the International Comprehensive Ocean-Atmosphere Dataset (ICOADS) reveals a simpler pattern of sea surface warming throughout the 20th century, including a reduced warming rate in the early 20th century and a less significant hiatus in the mid 20th century. The spatial pattern of warming also becomes more homogeneous, suggesting reduced importance of internal variability in driving climate variations in the last century. Improved quantification of data biases and errors also brings observational SST estimates into consistency with some CMIP historical simulations, increasing our confidence in future projections made by these climate models.
