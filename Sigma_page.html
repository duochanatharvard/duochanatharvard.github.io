Background

In addition to an increasing climatology of temperature, monthly temperature variability also sets a background for synoptic extreme hot events. It is, therefore, important to understand how monthly temperature variance will change in a warming climate. Current models projections of changes in summertime temperature variability, however, are very uncertain. To reduce the uncertainty, Fischer et al (2012) showed that models having greater variability in the current climate tend to have less increase of variability in the future. This is because these models are relatively dry in the current climate and have less space to further dry out in a warming climate.

Our Work

  • My colleagues (met at 2017 ACDC summer school) and I show that changes in summertime temperature variability increases with summertime global warming. Changes in temperature gradient and local soil moisture may be important mechanisms contributing to this relationship.

  • My colleagues at Harvard and I developed a diagnostic framework based on surface energy balance, which can be used to assess contributions from individual processes, e.g., insolation, soil moisture, etc., to changes in temperature variability. We revealed a region-dependent control for discrepancies among CMIP5 model projections.

  • In CMIP5 experiments, models having greater temperature variability in the current climate do not show less increase as previously expected. Instead, models show greater temperature variability show even greater increases. Using the framework developed in Chan et al (2019b), we show that effective drying of CMIP5 models does not depend on their current state. In addition, the non-linear dependency of temperature variability on soil moisture makes changes increase with current variability. This provides potentials for emergent constraints.
