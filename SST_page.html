SST Reconstruction



Sea-surface temperature (SST) is a crucial quantity for studying weather and climate. Current estimates of historical SSTs, however, are insufficient due to complicated SST biases and simplified corrections. To refine existing SST corrections, we develop a statistical framework to compare SSTs from ships that crossed nearby one another and to estimate systematic offsets between groups of SST records (Chan & Huybers 2019). We then use physical and historical evidence to understand the origin of these statistically constrained biases (Chan & Huybers, 2020a, historical document). Correcting groupwise SST biases resolves model-data discrepancies, including heterogenous early-twentieth-century warming (Chan et al., 2019) and warm SSTs during World War II (Chan & Huybers, 2020b). Corrections also permit better hindcasting of Atlantic hurricanes (Chan et al., 2020).

Background of SST biases and corrections

Sea-surface temperature (SST), typically defined at a depth of 20--30 cm of the ocean, is a crucial quantity for studying the earth's climate. Estimates of historical SSTs to an accuracy of 0.05°C at the global scale and 0.1°C at regional scales are required for a wide range of climate applications, which include depicting past climate change, attributing anthropogenic versus internal climate variability, and understanding changes in climate and weather events that have far-reaching societal impacts, such as El Nino and hurricanes.

Existing SST products based on in-situ instrumental observations, such as HadSSTs and ERSSTs, are all based on the International Comprehensive Ocean-Atmosphere Data Set (ICOADS). Before the large-scale deployment of buoys and drifters in the 1980s, SSTs compiled under the ICOADS were sampled using a variety of crude methods, such as buckets and engine-room intakes, by nonexpert sailors when they traveled across the ocean.


How to measure SSTs with a bucket? Ocean Weather Ship Record B (1947) from Youtube

These ship-based SSTs are full of biases and errors. For example, bucket measurements can be biased cold from wind evaporations or biased warm from solar heating. When averaged over seasons and the globe, bucket SSTs are estimated to be coldly biased by several tenths of degrees Celsius (Folland & Parker, 1995). The bias of individual bucket SSTs can be as large as ± 1°C under extreme winter and summer conditions (e.g., Kennedy et al., 2011). On the other hand, ERI SSTs are estimated to be biased warm from 0.1 to 0.3°C due to the heat released from ship engines (Kennedy et al., 2011). Taking into account that the estimated global warming in the 20th century is less than 1°C, correcting for these biases appears to be crucial.

Yet accurate accounting for biases in ship-based SSTs is not easy because biases depend not only on measurement methods but also instrument designs (e.g., types of buckets), measurement protocols, and postprocessing practices. For example, water temperature in a less-insulated canvas bucket can be additionally cooler than that in a wooden bucket by more than 0.5°C even measured under the same condition (Folland & Parker, 1995). In addition, buckets that stay on ship decks longer before measurements are taken will generally yield colder SSTs due to increased aerial exposure. As a result, biases of different subsets of SSTs coming from the same method can be distinct from one another.

However, limited metadata to constrain detailed measurement characteristics have necessitated simplified corrections in existing SST products. Existing corrections are often performed to the level of measurement methods, assuming that SSTs coming from the same method are biased in the same way. These corrections often use fixed patterns and slow-varying amplitudes to represent SST biases, which inevitably leads to insufficient corrections, especially at regional scales.

Our work on correcting SST biases

Our goal is to achieve refined regional SST corrections. Regional biases can arise from distinct biases associated with different subsets of SSTs and varying sampling coverage of these subsets. On account that ships from the same nation and data-collecting group would have used similar instruments, followed similar protocols, and experienced similar postprocessing practices, we correct SSTs for individual groups assigned by nation and ICOADS deck numbers, where the "deck" is the primary field to track ICOADS data collection and postprocessing.

  • In a method paper (Chan & Huybers, 2019), we develop a linear-mixed-effect (LME) model estimates systematic SST offsets among nation and data collecting groups. The LME method relies on inter-comparing nearby measurements from different groups. After applying the methodology to intercompare bucket SSTs, we find significant (P<0.05) differences among major collecting nations.

  • Correcting for groupwise offsets for bucket SST leads to more-homogeneous early 20th century SST warming (Chan et al., 2019), indicating the dominating role of anthropogenic activities, compared with natural variability, in the early 20th century. Correcting relatively warm German SSTs decreases the warming in the North Atlantic, whereas adjusting a drop in Japanese SSTs in the 1930s increases the warming in the North Pacific. The drop in Japanese temperatures arose from truncating temperatures to whole degrees Celsius. See a U.S. Air Force documentation for what happened to Japanese Kobe collection deck 118.

  • In Chan and Huybers (2020a), we use physical evidence (i.e., the amplitude of diurnal cycles) to understand the origin of LME groupwise offsets. We find that that offsets between bucket groups after the 1930s mainly arise from misclassifying ERI measurements as bucket SSTs. Groupwise offsets before the 1930s have a smaller range and should arise from physical differences in bucket design or measurement protocols. In addition to the amplitude of diurnal cycles, we extend a thermodynamical bucket model (here) to understand differences in bucket bias and diurnal amplitude under a wide parameter regime.

    • Fig. 3. Slopes between groupwise offsets and amplitudes of diurnal cycles. a & b, Groupwise offsets against diurnal amplitudes of individual bucket groups (filled markers) for a, 1910--1929 and for b, 1970--1989. Slopes (magenta lines) are estimated using bucket groups and a York regression technique. Whereas positive or uncertain slopes are found before the 1930s, significant negative slopes are found afterward (panel c). The offset-amplitude relationship changed sign in the 1930s, which was the advent time of engine room intake measurements in ICOADS. Also note that in panel b, the York regression slope passes through the independently determined diurnal amplitude and groupwise offset of ERI SSTs (double circles). These results suggest a prevalent misclassification of ERI SSTs, as coming from buckets, in data after the 1930s. Please refer to Chan and Huybers (2020a) for more details.

    In Chan and Huybers (2020b), we apply the LME method to intercompare all available ship-based SSTs in ICOADS and quantify warm offsets in U.S. and U.K. Naval ships during WWII. We also identify a significant warm anomaly in nighttime bucket SSTs during the war, possibly related to reading temperatures inside ships to avoid detection. Accounting for groupwise offsets more fully removes data heterogeneity and leads to the removal of a warm spike during WWII seen in ERSST5 and HadSST4. Our groupwise correction makes SSTs during WWII to agree with coastal and island station temperatures, as well as the range of internal variability in CMIP5 models.
