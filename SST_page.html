SST Reconstruction



Background

Ocean covers more than 70% of the earth surface and sea surface temperature (SST) is crucial for understanding global mean surface temperature (GMST). Existing instrumental SST products, e.g., HadSST3 and ERSST5, are all based on the International Comprehensive Ocean and Atmosphere Dataset (ICOADS). Before the deployment of buoys in the 1980s, SST in ICOADS mainly come from ships using variety of approaches, including bucket and engine room intake methods. These methods are not in-situ and are subject to biases. For example, bucket measurements can be coldly biased by more than -0.5°C because of evaporative and turbulent heat losses (Folland & Parker, 1995).

Correcting for these biases are crucial because the overall global warming is less than 1°C during the 20th century, whereas existing global SST corrections can be as large as 0.4°C (Kennedy et al., 2012). In addition, contributions from individual methods to the SST archive change with time, leading to time-varying biases. Besides the overall increasing global temperature, existing SST corrections also greatly change the inter-decadal variability of SST (Luke et al., 2018). Existing SST corrections only explicitly account for biases associated with different measurement methods and often assume that SST biases are smooth in space and time because of limited metadata for detailed corrections.

Our Work

  • Ships coming from different nations and sailing for different purposes could use different measurement methods or follow distinct protocols and have different bias structures. If so, SST biases will be regional and can vary abruptly in time. In a methodological paper (Chan & Huybers, 2019), we develop a Linear-mixed-effect model to inter-compare proximal bucket SSTs coming from different nation-deck groups. We detect significant offsets among major collecting nations and suggest potentials for more detailed corrections to improve instrumental SST reconstructions.

  • Correcting for group-wise SST offsets leads to more-homogeneous early 20th century SST warming (Chan et al., 2019). See ICOADSb in the figure below. Correcting for offsets in German measurements decreases warming rates in the North Atlantic, whereas correcting for Japanese offsets leads to increased and more uniform warming in the North Pacific. Japanese offsets in the 1930s primarily result from records having been truncated to whole degrees Celsius when digitized in the 1960s.
