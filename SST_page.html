SST Reconstruction



Background

Ocean covers more than two-thirds of the earth surface and sea surface temperature (SST) is crucial for monitoring climate state, understanding climate variability, and predicting climate changes. Existing SST products based on in-situ instrumental observations, e.g., HadSST3 and ERSST5, are all based on the International Comprehensive Ocean and Atmosphere Dataset (ICOADS). However, before the wide deployment of buoys and drifters in the 1980s, ICOADS SSTs mainly come from sailors using variety of methods while voyaging across the ocean. These methods mainly include bucket and engine room intake approaches and are, strictly speaking, not taking in-situ measurements because water temperatures were measured in buckets on ship decks or in ship's engine rooms.


How to measure SSTs with a bucket? Ocean Weather Ship Record B (1947) from Youtube

Ship-based SST measurements are full of biases and errors. For example, bucket measurements can be coldly biased from wind evaporations or warmly biased from solar heating. On average, bucket SSTs are coldly biased by several tenth of degree Celsius (Folland & Parker, 1995) but the biases can be as large as ± 1°C Under extreme winter and summer conditions (e.g., Kennedy et al, 2019). Taking into account that the estimated global warming in the 20th century is less than 1°C, correcting for these biases are, therefore, crucial for every tenth of a °C.

Accurate corrections of bucket SST biases, however, are difficult because the amount of SSTs from distinct methods change with time, leading to time-varying biases. Existing SST corrections often explicitly account for biases associated with distinct measurement methods, assuming that a US bucket SST will have the same bias as a Japanese bucket SST. These correction schemes also usually assume spatially and temporally smooth biases because of limited metadata for detailed corrections. However, buckets are made of different materials, come in different sizes, and can be used by different nations following different protocols. All these factors will affect temperature readings, and it may be important to correct for ocean temperatures for individual nations or other more refined groupings.

Our Work on correcting SST biases

We aim to achieve more refined SST corrections for individual nations (or other groupings) by first detecting groupwise SST biases using statistical approaches and find origins of these biases using physical modeling and evidence in historical documents.

For a quick story about correcting groupwise SST biases, please check the vedio below. Please also feel free to scroll down and learn about position errors of early ship tracks.

  • In a methodological paper (Chan & Huybers, 2019), we develop a linear-mixed-effect (LME) model to inter-compare bucket SSTs that came from different nation-deck groups but were taken at nearby spatial and temporal locations. We apply this method to more than 17 millions of SSTs and, for the first time, detect significant offsets among major collecting nations and suggest potentials for more detailed corrections to improve instrumental SST reconstructions.

    • Scheme for pairing in the LME intercomparison method

  • Correcting for group-wise bucket SST offsets leads to more-homogeneous early 20th century SST warming (Chan et al., 2019), indicating the dominating role of anthropogenic activities, compared with natural variabilities, even in the early 20th century. Correcting for offsets in German measurements decreases warming rates in the North Atlantic, whereas correcting for Japanese offsets leads to increased and more uniform warming in the North Pacific. Japanese offsets in the 1930s primarily result from records been truncated to whole degrees Celsius when digitized in the 1960s. See evidence in the reference manual for the Japanese Kobe Collection deck 118.

    • Fig. 1. 1908-1941 SST trends for a. ICOADS without group-wise corrections (ICOADSa), b. group-wise corrections, and c. ICOADS groupwise corrections (ICOADSb).

    Fig. 2. 1908-1941 SST anomalies over a. the North Pacific and b. the North Atlantic in a variety of observational SST estimates. Among which ICOADSb is the estimate with group-wise corrections.

  • In Chan and Huybers (2020), we show that whereas groupwise offsets before the 1930s should mainly reflect physical differences in bucket design or measurement protocols, larger groupwise offsets found after the 1930s are mainly due to misclassifying engine room intake measurements as bucket observations. Evidence comes from analysising the amplitude of diurnal cycles and simulations from an extended physical bucket model.

    • Fig. 3. Slopes between groupwise offsets and amplitudes of diurnal cycles across individual groups ( markers in a and b). Whereas positive slopes with large uncertainties are found before the 1930s, significant negative slopes are found afterwards (panel c). The change in the offset-amplitude relationship is in 1930, which is also the start of engine room intake measurements. Also note that the line of york fit regression across all bucket groups (magenta) passes through the independently determined diurnal amplitude and SST offsets for ERI measurements (double circles) after the 1930s (panel b). These results suggest that misclassification of ERI measurements is the simpliest explaination of groupwise offsets after the 1930s. Please refer to our paper for more details.
